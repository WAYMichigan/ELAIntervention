What Is a Counterargument — and Why Include It?
A strong argument doesn't ignore the other side. It addresses it directly and shows why your position is still correct. This is called a counterargument — and it actually makes your argument stronger, not weaker.
📌 Two Parts Every Time

Part 1 — Acknowledge: Show you understand why someone might disagree. Be fair and accurate — don't make the other side sound ridiculous.

Part 2 — Rebut: Explain why your argument is still correct, or why the opposing point doesn't outweigh your evidence.

Think of it like a debate: you're not pretending the other side doesn't exist. You're showing that you've considered it — and you still believe you're right.

✗ Weak Counterargument (dismissive)

Thesis: Schools should start later.

"Some people say schools shouldn't start later. But they're wrong because students need more sleep."

Why weak: Dismisses the other side without acknowledging any valid point. Doesn't show understanding of the opposing argument.

✓ Strong Counterargument

Thesis: Schools should start later.

Part 1: Critics of later start times argue that changing school schedules disrupts family routines, particularly for working parents who depend on current dismissal times for childcare.

Part 2: However, this logistical challenge does not outweigh the documented academic and health benefits of later starts. School districts that have shifted times report solutions such as adjusted after-school programs and staggered sibling schedules — suggesting the disruption is manageable, while the benefits to student health are not.

✦ Counterargument Frame (Two-Part)

Critics might argue that [opposing position]. This concern is understandable because [why their point has some validity]. However, [why your argument is still stronger / what evidence contradicts their claim / what they're missing].

Task 1 — Write Your Full Counterargument
Use your resilience thesis from the Centric project. Write a two-part counterargument using the frame above. Both parts must be in your response.
💡 Model (thesis: resilience is a skill anyone can develop): Critics might argue that resilience is largely determined by genetics and early childhood experience — that some people are simply wired to handle adversity better than others, and that trying to teach resilience to everyone is unrealistic. This concern is understandable because there is genuine research showing that stress response systems have biological components, and early trauma can have lasting psychological effects. However, this does not mean resilience cannot be developed — it means the starting point varies. Dr. Carol Dweck's growth mindset research and countless real-world examples, including Malala Yousafzai and the character Marcus, demonstrate that deliberate choices and practiced behaviors can build resilience regardless of where someone starts.
Task 2 — Self-Evaluate
Answer these two questions about your counterargument.
1. Did you represent the opposing view fairly — or did you make it sound ridiculous? 2. Did your rebuttal use evidence or reasoning, or did it just reassert your original claim?
