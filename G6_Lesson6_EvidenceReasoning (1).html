What's the Difference Between Evidence and Reasoning?
A lot of writers make the mistake of dropping evidence and walking away. Evidence alone doesn't prove anything. It's your reasoning — the explanation of how the evidence connects to your claim — that actually makes the argument.
📌 The Difference

Evidence — a specific fact, quote, statistic, or example that supports your claim. It comes from a source outside your own opinion.

Reasoning — your explanation of HOW the evidence proves your point. This is where your thinking goes.

Think of it this way: evidence is the hammer. Reasoning is the swing. Without the swing, the nail doesn't move.

✗ Evidence Without Reasoning (incomplete)

Claim: Resilience can be developed through practice.

Evidence only: "Dr. Dweck's research shows that students with a growth mindset outperform those with a fixed mindset."

Why incomplete: The evidence is good, but the writer never explains how this proves that resilience can be developed. The connection is assumed, not argued.

✓ Evidence + Reasoning (complete)

Claim: Resilience can be developed through practice.

Evidence: "Dr. Dweck's research shows that students with a growth mindset outperform those with a fixed mindset."

Reasoning: This supports the claim because a growth mindset — the belief that abilities improve through effort — is itself a form of resilience. If students can be taught to adopt this mindset and it measurably improves their performance, then resilience is not a fixed trait but a teachable skill.

✦ Evidence + Reasoning Frame (SRSD)

One piece of evidence that supports this claim is [evidence — quote, fact, or example] (from [source]). This supports my argument because [explain the logical connection]. In other words, [restate in simpler terms why this proves your point].

Build Two Evidence + Reasoning Responses

The Claim

Your thesis from Module 2: Use the thesis statement you wrote in the Centric project, OR use this one:

✦ Evidence + Reasoning Frame

One piece of evidence that supports this claim is "[quote or fact]" (from [source]). This supports my argument because [connection to claim]. In other words, [restate in simpler terms].

Task 1 — Evidence from the Literary Text
Use a quote or detail from Survival at Mile Marker 7 (or your Hatchet excerpt). Complete the full frame.
💡 Model: One piece of evidence from the literary text is "It was one thing, right now, that he could actually do" (Survival at Mile Marker 7). This supports my argument because Marcus — a character with no survival training — is actively choosing to focus on an immediate, achievable task rather than being paralyzed by fear. In other words, resilience here isn't a trait he was born with; it's a choice he makes in the moment, which proves it can be practiced.
Task 2 — Evidence from the Informational Text or Video
Use a fact or detail from the Malala video or the Amara Osei profile. Complete the full frame.
💡 Model: One piece of evidence from the Malala video is her statement that education is "the most powerful weapon" she could use to change the world. This supports my argument because Malala did not begin life with political power or resources — she developed her resilience deliberately, by choosing to continue attending school and speaking publicly despite explicit threats. In other words, her resilience was built action by action, not given to her.
