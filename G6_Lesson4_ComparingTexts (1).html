How Do You Compare Two Texts?
Comparing texts isn't just spotting what's similar or different on the surface. It's about analyzing how two authors approach the same theme — and what reading both together helps you understand that one alone wouldn't.
📌 The Framework

When comparing two texts on the same theme, ask three questions:

1. What does each text say about the theme? (Central idea)

2. How does each author develop the theme? (Genre, structure, evidence, character)

3. What do both texts together help you understand that one alone could not?

✦ Model Comparison

Text A (fiction): A story about a boy who keeps showing up to baseball practice despite losing every game of the season.

Text B (informational): An article about how setbacks in childhood build long-term resilience in adults.

Similarity: Both texts suggest that resilience grows through repeated difficulty, not through success.

Difference: Text A shows resilience through a character's actions and inner thoughts, while Text B supports the idea with research and expert opinion.

Together: Reading both makes the idea more convincing — the fiction makes it feel emotionally real, while the nonfiction proves it is scientifically grounded.

✦ Sentence Frame — Text Comparison

Both texts explore the theme of [theme], but they develop it differently. In [Text A], the author [how they develop the theme]. In [Text B], the author [how they develop the theme]. Together, they suggest that [what both texts together show].

We Do — Guided Practice
Read Both Texts, Then Answer
Read both short texts carefully. They share a theme but are different types of text.
📄 Text A — Fiction: "Still Standing" (excerpt)

Every time Nolan got knocked down in the ring, he got back up. Not because he thought he was going to win — he hadn't won a match in six weeks — but because lying on the mat was the only thing that felt like giving up. And Nolan had made a decision somewhere around the third week of losses that giving up was not something he was willing to do.

His coach watched from the corner and said very little. He had coached long enough to know that some lessons couldn't be taught. They could only be absorbed, slowly, through the body.

📄 Text B — Informational: "What Failure Teaches Us" (excerpt)

Psychologists studying resilience have found that one of the strongest predictors of long-term success is not talent or intelligence, but the ability to persist through repeated failure. Dr. Carol Dweck of Stanford University calls this a "growth mindset" — the belief that abilities can be developed through effort rather than fixed at birth.

Her research shows that students who are taught to view failures as learning opportunities consistently outperform those who are taught to avoid failure. The lesson, according to Dweck, is counterintuitive: struggling is not the opposite of learning. It is the process of learning.

Question 1 of 3
What theme do BOTH texts share?
Question 2 of 3
What is the most important DIFFERENCE between how the two texts develop this theme?
Question 3 of 3
What does reading BOTH texts together help you understand that one alone could not?
You Do — Independent Practice
Write Your Own Comparison
Read two new short texts below. Use the sentence frame to write a comparison paragraph.
📄 Text A — Fiction: "One More Try"

Fatima had failed her driver's test twice. Each time she walked out of the testing center she told herself it was the last time — and each time, three days later, she was back in the study guide. Her mother asked why she kept trying. Fatima thought for a moment. "Because I'm not done yet," she said.

📄 Text B — Informational: "The Science of Trying Again"

Research published in the Journal of Personality found that people who make multiple attempts at a goal are significantly more likely to eventually succeed than those who attempt it only once or twice. The key variable, researchers found, was not aptitude — it was the willingness to return after failure. Scientists call this "behavioral persistence," and it is one of the most reliable predictors of long-term achievement.

✦ Sentence Frame

Both texts explore the theme of [theme], but they develop it differently. In [Text A], the author [how]. In [Text B], the author [how]. Together, they suggest that [insight from both].

Task — Write Your Comparison Paragraph
Use the sentence frame to write a 3–5 sentence comparison of the two texts above.
💡 Model: Both texts explore the theme of persistence through failure, but they develop it differently. In "One More Try," the author uses a fictional character's quiet determination to show what persistence looks like from the inside — as a personal choice made privately, not dramatically. In "The Science of Trying Again," the author uses research findings to show that this kind of behavior is not just admirable but statistically proven to produce results. Together, they suggest that persistence after failure is both a deeply human experience and a scientifically validated path to success.
